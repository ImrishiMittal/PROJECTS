GEOGRAPHICAL LOCATION

Geographical Location of Rajasthan

Rajasthan, located in the northwestern part of India, is the largest state by area, covering approximately 342,239 square kilometers, which constitutes about 10.4% of the total geographical area of the country. The state is known for its diverse landscapes, ranging from arid deserts to fertile plains.

Boundaries and Neighbors
  • Northern Border: Punjab and Haryana.
  • Eastern Border: Uttar Pradesh.
  • Southeastern Border: Madhya Pradesh.
  • Southwestern Border: Gujarat.
  • Western Border: Shares an international boundary of approximately 1,070 km with Pakistan.
Key Geographical Features
1. The Thar Desert
  • Also known as the Great Indian Desert, it occupies a significant portion of western Rajasthan.
  • Characterized by sand dunes, sparse vegetation, and extreme climate.
  • Major cities in the desert region include Jaisalmer, Bikaner, and Barmer.
2. The Aravalli Range
  • One of the oldest mountain ranges in the world, running diagonally across the state from Mount Abu in the southwest to Khetri in the northeast.
  • Acts as a climatic barrier, dividing Rajasthan into the arid western region and the relatively fertile eastern plains.
  • Guru Shikhar, located in Mount Abu, is the highest peak in the state, at 1,722 meters.
3. Water Bodies
  • Rivers: The state is home to several rivers, including:
    • Chambal River: The only perennial river in Rajasthan, flowing in the southeastern region.
    • Banas River, Luni River, and Ghaggar River: Seasonal rivers.
  • Lakes: Known for its picturesque lakes like Lake Pichola (Udaipur), Sambhar Salt Lake, and Pushkar Lake.
4. Climate
  • Predominantly arid and semi-arid, with extreme temperature variations:
    • Summers: Can exceed 48°C in some regions.
    • Winters: Can drop to below 0°C in certain areas, especially near the Aravallis.
5. Vegetation and Wildlife
  • Sparse vegetation in desert areas; thorny bushes and xerophytes are common.
  • Wildlife sanctuaries like Ranthambore National Park, Sariska Tiger Reserve, and Keoladeo National Park are significant ecological zones.
Conclusion

Rajasthan's geographical diversity plays a pivotal role in shaping its history, culture, and lifestyle. From the golden sands of the Thar Desert to the lush valleys of the Aravallis, the state is a land of contrasts and natural beauty.
