LANGUAGES

Manipur is a linguistically diverse state, where several languages are spoken by its different ethnic communities. The languages of Manipur can be broadly classified into two categories: Scheduled Languages (recognized by the Constitution of India) and Other Languages (spoken by various tribal and indigenous groups). Here's a look at the key languages of Manipur:
1. Meitei (Manipuri)
  • Meitei (also called Manipuri) is the official language of Manipur and is spoken by the majority of the population, particularly the Meitei community, who are the dominant ethnic group in the state.
  • It belongs to the Kuki-Chin-Mizo group of languages in the Tibeto-Burman family.
  • Meitei is written in the Meitei Mayek script, which has ancient roots, though it was traditionally written in the Bengali script before the revival of Meitei Mayek in recent years.
  • The language is used for daily communication, in government, education, and media.
2. Nagamese
  • Nagamese is a creole language that developed as a lingua franca between different ethnic groups in the northeastern region of India, including in Manipur.
  • It is primarily a pidginized form of Assamese mixed with words and influences from other languages such as Meitei, Hindi, and Bengali.
  • Nagamese is commonly used in trade and communication, especially in the urban areas of Manipur.
3. Tribal Languages Manipur is home to a number of indigenous tribal communities, each with its own distinct language. Some of the prominent tribal languages include:
  • Thadou: Spoken by the Thadou-Kuki community, this language is part of the Kuki-Chin-Mizo language family.
  • Kuki: A broad group of languages spoken by various tribes under the Kuki ethnic group, each of which has its own dialect.
  • Poula: Spoken by the Pulai community in the hill districts.
  • Anal: A language spoken by the Anal tribe in the state’s southern region.
  • Tangkhul: Spoken by the Tangkhul Naga community in the northeastern parts of Manipur.
  • Rongmei: Spoken by the Rongmei Naga community, primarily in the northern region of Manipur.
  • Maram: Spoken by the Maram Naga people in the central parts of the state.
4. Hindi and English
  • Hindi is widely understood and spoken, especially in urban areas and by the migrant population. It is often used as a second language in daily communication and in educational institutions.
  • English is the medium of instruction in many schools and colleges in Manipur, especially in urban areas, and is widely used in official and administrative contexts.
Conclusion: Manipur’s linguistic diversity reflects its rich cultural heritage, with Meitei serving as the central language in the valley, while a host of tribal languages are spoken in the hilly regions. The coexistence of these languages enriches the social fabric of the state, creating a complex but harmonious linguistic environment.
