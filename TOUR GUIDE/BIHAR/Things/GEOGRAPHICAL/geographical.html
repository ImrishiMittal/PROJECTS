GEOGRAPHICAL LOCATION

Bihar is located in the eastern part of India, bordered by Nepal to the north and several Indian states to the south, east, and west. Its geographical coordinates are approximately 25.0961° N latitude and 85.3131° E longitude. Here’s a detailed overview of Bihar’s geographical location:

Borders
  • North: Bihar shares an international border with Nepal, stretching approximately 600 kilometers. This border is characterized by rivers and hills, contributing to a diverse ecosystem.
  • South: The state is bordered by Uttar Pradesh to the south.
  • East: Bihar shares its eastern boundary with West Bengal, which is separated by the Ganges River.
  • West: To the west, Bihar is bordered by Uttar Pradesh.
Major Rivers
  • Ganges: Bihar is home to several significant rivers, most notably the Ganges, which flows from west to east through the state, providing water for agriculture and sustaining the livelihoods of millions.
  • Brahmaputra: Flows through the northeastern region of the state.
  • Sone River: A major tributary of the Ganges, it flows through the southwestern part of Bihar.
  • Gandak and Kosi: These rivers are also significant in terms of irrigation and flood control.
Topography
  • Plains: The majority of Bihar is characterized by flat plains, which are fertile and suitable for agriculture. The landscape is predominantly alluvial, formed by the sediment deposited by rivers.
  • Hills: The northern part of the state features the Siwalik Hills, which are part of the lower Himalayan range. These hills provide a contrast to the otherwise flat terrain of the state.
  • Flood-prone Areas: Bihar experiences frequent flooding due to the annual monsoon rains and the overflowing of rivers, particularly the Ganges and its tributaries.
Climate
  • Winter: (December to February) Mild and dry, with temperatures ranging from 5°C to 20°C.
  • Summer: (March to June) Hot and dry, with temperatures often exceeding 40°C.
  • Monsoon: (June to September) Characterized by heavy rainfall, this season is crucial for agriculture.
  • Post-Monsoon: (October to November) Transition period with cooler temperatures and occasional rainfall.
Flora and Fauna
  • Valmiki National Park: Located in the west, it is home to a variety of wildlife, including tigers and elephants.
  • Bhimbandh Wildlife Sanctuary: Located in the Munger district, it is known for its diverse ecosystems and wildlife.
Conclusion

Bihar's geographical location, with its fertile plains, significant river systems, and climatic conditions, plays a vital role in shaping the state's agricultural economy and cultural practices. The interaction between its topography and climate has influenced the livelihoods of its people, making Bihar a land rich in natural resources and cultural heritage.
