TRADITION

Assam is a land rich in traditions, deeply rooted in its historical past and cultural diversity. The state, with its harmonious blend of indigenous tribes, ethnic communities, and cultural influences from neighboring regions, presents a unique and vibrant tradition. From its traditional festivals to art, dance, music, and crafts, Assam has preserved its customs while adapting to modern times.
1. Festivals:
  • Bihu: There are three forms of Bihu celebrated in Assam—Rongali Bihu, Bhogali Bihu, and Kongali Bihu. Rongali Bihu, celebrated in April, marks the Assamese New Year and the arrival of spring. It is associated with dance, music, and feasting. Bhogali Bihu, celebrated in January, is the harvest festival, where people enjoy community feasts and celebrate a good harvest. Kongali Bihu, observed in October, is a more solemn affair, focusing on prayers for a good harvest in the future.
  • Ambubachi Mela: This is an important religious festival celebrated at the Kamakhya Temple in Guwahati. It marks the fertility of the earth and attracts thousands of pilgrims from across India.
  • Brahmaputra Festival: A modern festival celebrating the cultural diversity of Assam, held along the banks of the Brahmaputra River, showcasing local arts, crafts, cuisine, and performances.
  • Jonbeel Mela: A unique festival that revives the ancient barter system, Jonbeel Mela is held in the Morigaon district, where people from different tribes and communities exchange goods in a traditional manner.
2. Traditional Attire:
  • Mekhela Chador: This is the traditional attire for Assamese women. It consists of two pieces—the mekhela, a cylindrical skirt worn from the waist down, and the chador, a draped cloth worn over the upper body. The garment is usually made of silk, with the famous Muga silk and Pat silk being preferred for special occasions.
  • Dhoti and Gamosa: Men traditionally wear a dhoti, a long piece of cloth tied around the waist, paired with a chador or kurta. The gamosa, a handwoven cotton towel, holds cultural significance in Assam and is used in traditional rituals, offered as a mark of respect, or worn around the neck as a symbol of Assamese pride.
3. Music and Dance:
  • Bihu Dance: The most popular folk dance of Assam, Bihu Dance, is performed during Rongali Bihu. It is characterized by energetic movements, rhythmical swaying of the hips, and vibrant music. Both men and women participate in this dance, wearing colorful traditional attire.
  • Sattriya Dance: An Indian classical dance form that originated in the Vaishnavite monasteries (Satras) of Assam, Sattriya was introduced by the saint-scholar Srimanta Sankardeva in the 15th century. This dance is performed as part of Bhakti (devotional) performances in monasteries, and it is also presented on stage today.
  • Husori and Zikir: Traditional folk music forms like Husori (performed during Bihu) and Zikir (songs influenced by Sufi and Islamic traditions) are also integral to Assamese tradition. These songs often revolve around themes of love, devotion, and nature.
4. Arts and Crafts:
  • Weaving: Weaving is a time-honored tradition in Assam. Almost every household in rural Assam has a loom, and Assamese women take pride in weaving their own clothes. The state is famous for its production of Muga silk, known for its durability and golden sheen, as well as Eri silk and Pat silk.
  • Cane and Bamboo Craft: The abundant availability of bamboo and cane in Assam has led to the flourishing of bamboo crafts. Artisans create a wide variety of products, from furniture and baskets to intricately designed decorative items. Jaapi (traditional sun hat) made from bamboo and palm leaves is a symbol of Assamese hospitality and culture.
  • Mask Making: The tradition of mask making, especially in Majuli, is a significant part of Assamese cultural heritage. These masks are used in religious plays like the Ankiya Naat, a traditional Vaishnavite drama, and are made from materials like bamboo, clay, and cloth.
5. Religion and Spirituality:
  • Vaishnavism: The Vaishnavite tradition, introduced by Srimanta Sankardeva in the 15th century, holds a significant place in Assam's religious and cultural life. Sankardeva’s Bhakti Movement led to the establishment of Satras (Vaishnavite monasteries), which became centers for religious, cultural, and social activities.
  • Shaktism: Assam is also an important center for Shaktism, with the Kamakhya Temple being one of the most revered Shakti Peethas in India. The temple is a focal point for the worship of the goddess Kamakhya and attracts pilgrims and tourists alike.
  • Tribal Traditions: Assam is home to a large number of indigenous tribes, each with its own traditional practices, rituals, and beliefs. Tribes like the Bodos, Karbi, Mishing, and Dimasa celebrate their unique festivals and follow age-old customs related to nature worship and ancestor worship.
6. Cuisine:
  • Assamese cuisine: is an integral part of the state's tradition. It is known for its simplicity, use of local ingredients, and a balance of flavors. Traditional dishes include rice, fish curry, pithas (rice cakes), and dishes made with bamboo shoots, mustard, and herbs. Assamese meals are often served on bell metal plates and bowls, which is a cultural practice in many households.
Conclusion:
The traditions of Assam are a reflection of its rich cultural history, deeply rooted in its agrarian past, indigenous customs, and religious beliefs. From its vibrant festivals and traditional dance forms to its unique crafts and simple yet flavorful cuisine, Assam's traditions continue to thrive, contributing to its unique identity in India’s cultural mosaic.
